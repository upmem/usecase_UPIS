'''Edward Parsons Smith''' (September 30, 1860 â€“ May 21, 1930) was the [[List of mayors of Omaha, Nebraska|mayor]] of [[Omaha]], [[Nebraska]] from 1918 to 1921.

==Biography==
Smith was born in [[Mount Pleasant, Iowa]] on September 30, 1860, and was a son of Edward Smith and Celia (Schockley) Smith.<ref name= Wakeley>{{cite book |last=Wakeley |first=Arthur Cooper |date=1917 |title=Omaha: The Gate City, and Douglas County, Nebraska |volume= 2 |url=https://archive.org/details/omahagatecitydou02wake |location=Chicago, IL |publisher=S.J. Clarke Publishing Company |page=[https://archive.org/details/omahagatecitydou02wake/page/448 448]-451}}</ref>  He attended the local schools and graduated from Mount Pleasant's Howe's Academy.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>  He graduated from the [[University of Iowa College of Law]] with an [[bachelor of laws|LL.B.]] degree in 1885, and was admitted to the bar.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>  Smith relocated to [[Seward, Nebraska]] in the summer of 1885, and established a law practice that specialized in interstate commerce.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>  He moved to Omaha in 1890 and continued to practice law.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>

In addition to his law practice, Smith was active in several businesses.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>  He was the attorney for the Omaha Grain Exchange, of which he was also a board of directors member.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>  In addition, Smith was vice president of the Omaha Cooperage Company, and served on its board of directors.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>

==Mayor of Omaha==
A [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democrat]],<ref name= "Wakeley"/> Smith ran for mayor in 1918 and beat incumbent Mayor "Cowboy" [[Jim Dahlman]] on a reform ticket aiming to defeat [[Tom Dennison (Political boss)|Tom Dennison]]'s political machine, which at that point had run Omaha for at least 15 years. Dennison's displeasure with Smith's morality stance was frequently voiced, and took shape in the [[Omaha Race Riot of 1919]]. During that riot Smith was strung up and almost [[lynched]] by a mob.<ref>[http://www.nebraskastudies.org/0700/frameset_reset.html?http://www.nebraskastudies.org/0700/resources/0700_1040.html Lesson Plans for Omaha Race Riot of 1919] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171225034916/http://www.nebraskastudies.org/0700/frameset_reset.html?http%3A%2F%2Fwww.nebraskastudies.org%2F0700%2Fresources%2F0700_1040.html |date=2017-12-25 }}. NebraskaStudies.org. Retrieved 12/28/07.</ref> After being rescued by [[Omaha Police Department|Omaha Police]] detectives, he was taken to Omaha's [[Ford Hospital]]. Smith's political interest never returned.

==Death and burial==
He died in Omaha on May 21, 1930.<ref>{{cite news |agency=United Press |date=May 22, 1930 |title=Ed P. Smith is Dead |url=https://www.newspapers.com/image/298203695/ |work=[[Lincoln Journal Star|Lincoln Evening Journal]] |location=Lincoln, NE |page=6 |url-access=subscription  |via=[[Newspapers.com]]}}</ref>  and was buried in Section 23, Lot 186 of [[Forest Lawn Memorial Park (Omaha)|Forest Lawn Memorial Park]] in Omaha.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.interment.net/data/us/ne/douglas/forestl/records-sib-smy.htm |title=Burial Record: Smith, Edward P. Died 5/21/1930 |website=Burial Records, Forest Lawn Memorial Park, Omaha, Douglas County, Nebraska |publisher=Interment.net |access-date=October 17, 2018}}</ref>

==Family==
In November, 1883 Smith married Margaret Wertman of [[Greenfield, Iowa]].<ref name= "Wakeley"/>  They were the parents of Ida B. Smith, Lisle W. Smith, and Edward Esher Smith.<ref name= "Wakeley"/>
