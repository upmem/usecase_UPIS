'''George Albert Soper II''' (2 February 1870 – 17 June 1948) was an American sanitation engineer. He was best known for discovering [[Mary Mallon]], also known as Typhoid Mary, a carrier of [[Typhoid fever]] who had no symptoms.<ref>[https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1911442/pdf/bullnyacadmed00595-0063.pdf GEORGE A.SOPER: THE CURIOUS CAREER OF TYPHOID MARY] ''www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov'', accessed 25 April 2020</ref>

== Biography ==
Soper was the son of George Albert Soper (1837 – 1869) and Georgianna Lydia Buckman (d. 1882).<ref name="FG_GAS" />

He received his degree from [[Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute]] in 1895 and a PhD from [[Columbia University]] in 1899.{{sfn|Leavitt|1996|p=15}} He was described as 'Major, US Army', in the entry for 1907 in the New York City Department of Health Centennial Newspaper noting the 'discovery of the carrier, Typhoid Mary'.<ref>[https://www1.nyc.gov/assets/doh/downloads/pdf/history/chronology-1966centennial.pdf Arthur Bushel, in 'Frank Leslie's Illustrated Newspaper' 1866 New York City Department of Health Centennial 1966, page 14] March 1966 ''www1.nyc.gov'', accessed 27 April 2020</ref> He was the managing director of the American Society for the Control of Cancer, which later changed its name to the [[American Cancer Society]], from 1923–1928.<ref name=FG_GAS>{{findagrave|110265766}}</ref>

Typhoid Mary was tracked down and arrested by Ms. Willa Carey Noble, a bacteriologist at ''Research laboratories of the Public Health Department of New York City'' under [[William Hallock Park]], in Mr. Soper's office, as he acknowledged in his writings.{{where|date=April 2020}}
