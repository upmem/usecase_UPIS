'''Neil Oliver''' (born 21 February 1967) is a Scottish<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.spectator.co.uk/article/a-british-state-of-mind|title=A British state of mind|date=29 November 2020|via=www.spectator.co.uk|access-date=30 November 2020|url-status=live}}</ref> television presenter, freelance archaeologist, conservationist, and author. He is best known as a presenter of several [[BBC]] historical and archaeological documentary series, including ''[[A History of Scotland (TV series)|A History of Scotland]]'', ''[[Vikings (TV documentary series)|Vikings]]'', and ''[[Coast (TV series)|Coast]]''. In 2017, he was appointed president of the [[National Trust for Scotland]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bbc.com/news/uk-scotland-41452552|title=TV historian takes on conservation role|date=30 September 2017|via=www.bbc.co.uk|access-date=9 August 2020|archive-date=28 December 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191228004317/https://www.bbc.com/news/uk-scotland-41452552|url-status=live}}</ref>

==Early life and education==
Oliver was born in [[Renfrewshire (historic)|Renfrewshire]] and grew up in [[Ayr]] and [[Dumfries]] where he attended [[Dumfries Academy]]. He then attended the [[University of Glasgow]]. He obtained an MA (Hons) and then worked as a freelance archaeologist, before training as a journalist.<ref Name="Pass/Fail">{{Cite web|url=http://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/profiles/passedfailed-an-education-in-the-life-of-neil-oliver-archaeologist-and-television-presenter-1014122.html|title=Passed/failed: An education in the life of Neil Oliver, archaeologist|date=13 November 2008|website=The Independent|access-date=9 August 2020|archive-date=12 April 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190412143236/https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/profiles/passedfailed-an-education-in-the-life-of-neil-oliver-archaeologist-and-television-presenter-1014122.html|url-status=live}}</ref>

==Filmography==
Oliver's television debut came in 2002 with [[BBC Two]]'s ''[[Two Men in a Trench]]'', which featured Oliver and close friend, [[Tony Pollard (archaeologist)|Tony Pollard]], visiting historic British battlefields and recreating the battle situations using state of the art archaeological techniques. In addition to the TV series, Oliver co-wrote the two accompanying books.

In 2005, he wrote a tie-in book for the [[Channel 4]] documentary, ''[[Not Forgotten (TV)|Not Forgotten]]'', which was presented by [[Ian Hislop]]. Oliver then became the archaeological and social history expert on ''[[Coast (TV series)|Coast]]'' and in the next series he replaced [[Nicholas Crane]] as the show's main presenter and remained as such for the third and fourth series.

In 2006, Oliver appeared in two more documentary series, Channel 4's ''The Face of Britain'' and BBC Two's ''Scotland's History: The Top Ten''. In August 2006, he also appeared on the special "Big Royal Dig" edition of Channel 4's ''[[Time Team]]'', in which he presented a dig at [[Holyrood Palace]].

Oliver was a contributor to [[BBC One]]'s ''[[The One Show]]'' in the summer of 2007. He also appeared that year as one of the presenters of BBC Two series ''The History Detectives''.

Oliver's series ''[[A History of Scotland]]'' began airing on 9 November 2008 on [[BBC One Scotland]] and was broadcast throughout the UK in 2009. The series also has links to radio, online and [[Open University]] materials. Like ''Coast'', the programme is a co-production of the BBC and the [[Open University]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/pressoffice/pressreleases/stories/2008/03_march/06/scotlandhistory.shtml|title=BBC - Press Office - Celebrating Scotland's History|website=www.bbc.co.uk|access-date=6 March 2008|archive-date=30 April 2008|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080430001021/http://www.bbc.co.uk/pressoffice/pressreleases/stories/2008/03_march/06/scotlandhistory.shtml|url-status=live}}</ref>

On 23 March 2009, Neil Oliver presented a programme on [[Cleopatra]] on BBC One.

In February 2011, he presented ''A History of Ancient Britain'' on BBC Two.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b00yk27f |title=Archived copy |access-date=9 February 2011 |archive-date=11 February 2011 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110211024446/http://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b00yk27f |url-status=live }}</ref> This was followed later in the year by ''A History of Celtic Britain''. He also provided the voice-over for [[VisitScotland]]'s 2011 television advertisement.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://visitscotland.briefyourmarket.com/Newsletters/VisitScotland-Local-eUpdate---March-2011-/New-VisitScotland-TV-advert.aspx|title=eUpdate: New VisitScotland TV advert|publisher=VisitScotland|date=March 2011|accessdate=9 July 2011|archive-date=26 March 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120326194113/http://visitscotland.briefyourmarket.com/Newsletters/VisitScotland-Local-eUpdate---March-2011-/New-VisitScotland-TV-advert.aspx|url-status=dead}}</ref> Oliver also hosted ''The Last Explorers'', a four-episode series retracing the expeditions of four Scottish explorers ([[David Livingstone]], [[William Speirs Bruce]], [[John Muir]], and [[Thomas Blake Glover]]) who planted ideas rather than flags, that screened on BBC for four weeks from 24 November 2011.

Oliver presented the three-part series ''[[Vikings (TV documentary series)|Vikings]]'' 11 September 2012 – 25 September 2012.

Oliver presented the three-part series ''[[Sacred Wonders of Britain]]'' for [[BBC Two]] and [[Coast (TV series)#Coast Australia|Coast Australia]], aired in 2013–14.

In 2015, he presented ''Britain's Deadliest Rail Disaster: Quintinshill'' about the [[Quintinshill rail disaster]], which took place in 1915.

In 2015, he co-presented a 3-part BBC TV documentary with [[Alice Roberts]], entitled ''The Celts: Blood, Iron and Sacrifice'',<ref name="BBC CELT">{{Cite web |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b06h7x5f |title=Archived copy |access-date=17 October 2015 |archive-date=8 October 2015 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151008024133/http://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b06h7x5f |url-status=live }}</ref> and the following year continued his ''Coast'' series with ''[[Coast New Zealand]]''.

Neil Oliver also presented a BBC documentary called ''Scotland and the Klan'', highlighting the Scottish foundations of the [[Ku Klux Klan]], originally aired in October 2016.

In December 2016, Oliver was involved in episode 2 of the Celebrity Series of ''[[Robot Wars (TV series)|Robot Wars]]'', helping create a robot called "Soldier ANT",(the initials of Neil and his two team members, sons Archie and Teddy). It won one game (on a judges' decision), but lost the other two on knockout and was eliminated.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.radiotimes.com/news/2016-12-02/robbie-savage-suzi-perry-and-the-brownlee-brothers-join-robot-wars-battle-of-the-stars-line-up/|title=Robbie Savage, Suzi Perry and the Brownlee brothers join Robot Wars: Battle of the Stars line-up|website=Radio Times|access-date=9 August 2020|archive-date=27 February 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180227000656/http://www.radiotimes.com/news/2016-12-02/robbie-savage-suzi-perry-and-the-brownlee-brothers-join-robot-wars-battle-of-the-stars-line-up/|url-status=live}}</ref>

In 2017, Neil Oliver co-presented BBC 4's 3-part history series ''Britain's Ancient Capital: Secrets of Orkney''. The other presenters were Chris Packham, Andy Torbet, and Shini Somara.

In December 2018, he presented BBC4's 3 part docu-drama series ''Rise of the Clans''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.heraldscotland.com/arts_ents/17286100.barry-didcocks-tv-review-rise-of-the-clans-bbc-one/|title=A new telling of an old favourite|website=HeraldScotland|access-date=22 February 2019|archive-date=23 February 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190223020423/https://www.heraldscotland.com/arts_ents/17286100.barry-didcocks-tv-review-rise-of-the-clans-bbc-one/|url-status=live}}</ref>

==Personal life==
He lives in [[Stirling]]<ref>''[[The Sarah Millican Television Programme]]'', 12 February 2013{{better source needed|date=August 2020}}</ref> with his wife Trudi and their three children.<ref>[http://women.timesonline.co.uk/tol/life_and_style/women/the_way_we_live/article6336971.ece A Life in the Day: Neil Oliver – Times Online]{{dl|date=August 2020}}</ref> He is a patron of the Association of [[Lighthouse]] Keepers.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://alk.org.uk/|title=Association of Lighthouse Keepers - Keeping Lighthouse Heritage Alive|website=Association of Lighthouse Keepers|access-date=9 August 2020|archive-date=24 October 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191024074316/https://alk.org.uk/|url-status=live}}</ref>

In November 2011, he was awarded an Honorary Doctorate of Letters by the [[Abertay University|University of Abertay Dundee]].<ref>{{cite web |author=Tel: +44 (0)1382 308000 Ask a question |url=http://www.abertay.ac.uk/about/news/pickoftheweek/2012/name,8964,en.html |title=2012 &#124; University of Abertay Dundee. |publisher=Abertay.ac.uk |date=25 September 2009 |accessdate=17 September 2012 |archive-date=22 November 2012 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121122062128/http://abertay.ac.uk/about/news/pickoftheweek/2012/name,8964,en.html |url-status=dead }}</ref>

==Politics==

In May 2014, Oliver stated in an interview with ''[[The Herald (Glasgow)|The Herald]]'', that he was "proud of Britain" and noted his dislike of the forthcoming [[2014 Scottish independence referendum|Scottish independence referendum]] saying that he found "this kind of internecine squabbling puts my teeth on edge. I would rather that it would just go away – or that it had never happened". He went on to say that he "liked the status quo".<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.heraldscotland.com/arts_ents/13160805.neil-oliver-on-the-search-for-the-site-of-the-battle-of-bannockburn/|title=Neil Oliver on the search for the site of the Battle of Bannockburn|website=HeraldScotland}}</ref> When he was appointed President of the [[National Trust for Scotland]], thousands signed petitions calling on him to resign.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.heraldscotland.com/news/15569410.video-hundreds-back-call-to-remove-divisive-unionist-tv-star-as-national-trust-for-scotland-president/|title=Video: Hundreds back call to remove "divisive" Unionist TV star as National Trust for Scotland president|website=HeraldScotland|access-date=9 August 2020|archive-date=5 April 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190405054542/https://www.heraldscotland.com/news/15569410.video-hundreds-back-call-to-remove-divisive-unionist-tv-star-as-national-trust-for-scotland-president/|url-status=live}}</ref>

In June 2020, in the midst of a row about his admiration for the historian [[David Starkey]], Neil Oliver announced he was resigning from the National Trust for Scotland board.<ref name="auto">{{Cite web|url=https://www.scotsman.com/news/people/neil-oliver-step-down-national-trust-scotland-role-days-after-twitter-row-2904517|title=Neil Oliver to step down from National Trust for Scotland role days after Twitter row|website=www.scotsman.com|access-date=6 July 2020|archive-date=6 July 2020|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200706063623/https://www.scotsman.com/news/people/neil-oliver-step-down-national-trust-scotland-role-days-after-twitter-row-2904517|url-status=live}}</ref> However, Oliver pointed out that he was 'stepping down “as intended” when his three year term in the role comes to an end in September [2020].' The National Trust for Scotland also dismissed claims that Oliver endorsed Starkey's controversial comments as "untrue".<ref name="auto"/>
