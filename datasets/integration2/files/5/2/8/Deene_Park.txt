{{short description|Country manor north-east of Corby in the county of Northamptonshire, England}}
[[File:Deene Park Northamptonshie.jpg|thumb|Deene Park from the southeast]]
[[File:Entrance to Deene Park, Deene, Northamptonshire - geograph.org.uk - 255773.jpg|thumb|The entrance gates]]
'''Deene Park''', the seat of the Brudenell family since 1514,<ref>Deene was purchased by [[Robert Brudenell (Chief Justice)|Chief Justice Sir Robert Brudenell]]; the earlier history of the Manor of Deene in Corby Hundred is traced in [[John Bridges (topographer)|John Bridges]], ''History and Antiquities of Northamptonshire'', II; the family's history is traced, based on the abundant Brudenell archives, by Joan Wake, ''The Brudenells of Deene'', 1953.</ref> is a country manor located {{convert|5|mi|km|0|abbr=off}} northeast of [[Corby]] in the county of [[Northamptonshire]], [[England]]. The hall itself is a [[Grade I listed]] building dating back to the 14th century which has been modified several times since then to create the current structure.<ref> {{NHLE|num=1040131|desc= DEENE HALL |accessdate= 28 October 2016}} </ref>

Seven of the Brudenell family were [[Earl of Cardigan|Earls of Cardigan]] — the most notable being the [[James Brudenell, 7th Earl of Cardigan|7th Earl]] who led the [[Charge of the Light Brigade]] at the [[Battle of Balaclava]] (1854). The 7th Earl died childless in 1868, and while the Earldom of Cardigan merged with the [[Marquess of Ailesbury|Marquessate of Ailesbury]], the Deene Park estate passed, after the death in 1915 of his widow, to the descendants of his second cousin [[Ernest Brudenell-Bruce, 3rd Marquess of Ailesbury]].<ref>[https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/obituaries/10292398/Marian-Brudenell.html Obituary of Marian Brudenell]</ref> 

The estate was inherited by its current owner, Robert Brudenell, in 2014; he is the son of Edmund and Marian Brudenell, who devoted their lives to the rehabilitation of Deene Park and are largely responsible for the estate's present appearance.<ref>[https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/obituaries/11229333/Edmund-Brudenell-obituary.html Obituary of Edmund Brudenell]</ref> They are descended from the [[Ernest Brudenell-Bruce, 3rd Marquess of Ailesbury|3rd Marquess]]'s son [[Commodore (Royal Navy)|Commodore]] Lord Robert Brudenell-Bruce [[Royal Navy|RN]]. Lord Robert's son George, Edmund's father, inherited the family seat along with the family's remaining estates in Leicestershire and Northamptonshire<ref>{{cite news|title=Edmund Brudenell - obituary|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/obituaries/11229333/Edmund-Brudenell-obituary.html|work=[[The Daily Telegraph]]|date=13 November 2014}}</ref> and reverted the family name back to "Brudenell" by Royal Licence.<ref>{{cite book|title=Burke's Peerage and Baronetage: 106th Edition|publisher=[[Burke's Peerage]]|date=1999|page=39|first=Charles|last=Mosley|author-link=Charles Mosley (genealogist)}}</ref>

==History==
[[File:Cardigan.jpg|thumb|left|150px|7th Earl of Cardigan]]
The manor of Deene belonged to [[Westminster Abbey]]; annual rent of £18 was paid until 1970.<ref>[http://www.deenepark.com/ Deene Park Website]</ref> The [[manor house]] has been expanded around its courtyard from its sixteenth-century core, represented by its [[great hall]], which was given its screen panelling and fireplace in 1571.<ref>the armorial fireplace, "executed in an up-to-date style of French origin", is illustrated in Nicholas Cooper, ''Houses of the Gentry 1480-1680'' 1999:267.</ref> The early 19th-century Bow Room contains the Brudenell library, collected in the 16th century by [[Thomas Tresham II|Sir Thomas Tresham]] and his son-in-law, Sir [[Earl of Cardigan|Thomas Brudenell, 1st Earl of Cardigan]], though the collection no longer includes the manuscript of Chaucer's ''[[Canterbury Tales]]''<ref>Clara Marburg, "Notes on the Cardigan Chaucer Manuscript" ''PMLA'', '''41'''.2 (June 1926:229-251); the ms was sold at Sotheby's 7 April 1925, and is now at the [[University of Texas]]</ref> or the last copy of [[Magna Carta]] in private hands.<ref>[http://www.sothebys.com/liveauctions/event/N08461_MagnaCarta.pdf Sotheby's sale catalogue, December 2007]</ref> 

The house contains furnishings of different periods and portraits, including works by [[Joshua Reynolds]] and [[Thomas Gainsborough]]. Relics of the [[Crimean War]] include the uniforms of Lord Cardigan and the head and tail of his [[Horses in warfare#Europe|charger]], Ronald.

The large gardens designed by [[David Nightingale Hicks]] feature a [[parterre]] overlooking the lake, and a newly planted avenue. 

The manor is located in the countryside not far from the [[Welland Viaduct|Harringworth Viaduct]] and is surrounded by historic villages and [[hamlet (place)|hamlets]]. [[St Peter's Church, Deene]], in the grounds, has the funeral monuments of the Brudenells.

Throughout the 1990s, Deene Park hosted the annual August [[bank holiday]] [[Greenbelt Festival]].
